The human microbiota has profound effects on health and disease.The presence of microbes is essential for health maintenance. It has been recognized that human genome is only part of our genetic composition while the human host and microbiota have coevolved. Also, the microbiome is intimately involved in the development and maintenance of the immune system. All these factors and advances in the sequencing technologies motivated the microbiome study. For the scope of this paper, we will focus on gut microbiota which represents the community of microorganisms that live in the digestive tracts of humans. We are interested in finding out how composition of gut microbiota is associated with the host's diet and the host's state of health or disease.

1 Introduction

The human microbiome is defined as the collection of microbes - bacteria, viruses, and single-cell eukaryotes - that inhabits the human body. In a healthy human adult microbes are estimated to outnumber human cells by a ratio of ten to one, and the total number of genes in the microbiome exceeds the number of genes in the human genome by a factor of at least 200. Despite the fact that microbial cells are only one-tenth to one-hundredth the size of a human cell, they may account for up to five pounds of adult body weight. Human body is home to approximately 100 trillion bacteria and one quadrillion viruses. All of these organisms perform a multitude of functions, and need to be properly balanced and taken care of in order to maintain good health. The identity and degree of diversity of these bacteria in a single individual, their variability from person to person, and their role in disease and disease susceptibility have crucial impact on human health. Human microbiome of an individual is as unique as finger print and is often referred to as second genome. To date, researchers have been able to study limited number of individual microorganisms. It has been impossible to isolate the vast majority (>95%) of microorganisms and culture them, because the required growth conditions cannot be reproduced in the laboratory. However, recent technological advances in DNA sequencing and the development of a method known as metagenomics have now made it feasible to analyze the entire human microbiome. Metagenomics enabled researchers to find out relative abundance of the different species and discover which metabolic pathways are encoded by the organisms to gain information about their functions in the body. As stated earlier, we are interested in the gut microbiota. Healthy gut flora is largely responsible for overall health of the host. The normal human gut microbiota comprises of two major phyla, namely Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes. The normal gut microbiota imparts specific function in host nutrient metabolism, drug metabolism, maintenance of structural integrity of the gut mucosal barrier, immunomodulation, and protection against pathogens.(Sai Manasa Jandhyala et al., 2015) As the gut microbiome in particular appears to be amenable to manipulation (Manichanh et al., 2010), an ecological understanding based on these studies may hold the potential for disease treatment and, perhaps most importantly, prevention through microbiome therapeutics. Studying the gut microbiota currently consists of two major stages: (1) 16S rRNA based sequencing of bacterial gene; and (2) Bioinformatics Analysis. For the scope of this paper, we are specifically interested in exploring the microbiota dynamics in 2 individuals over the period of time. We also aim to identify events such as diarrhea, travel or antibiotic use that could have caused some dramatic distortions.

2 Related Work

Human Microbiome Project is the major contributor to the technological enhancements and achievements in studying human microbiome. It was the extension of Human genome project and was funded by Nationa l Institutes of Health with the vision to identify and characterize the microorganisms that are found in the association with both healthy and diseased humans.(The NIH HMP Working Group ., 2009) It is said that a symbiotic relationship between intestinal microbiota and the host is established soon after birth. Many factors can affect a normal pattern of intestinal colonization such as mode of delivery, type of feeding, and antibiotic exposure.(Gronlund MM ., 1999) The gut microbiota have been associated with a large array of human diseases ranging from luminal diseases (Sai Manasa Jandhyala et al., 2015) such as inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD) and irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), metabolic diseases such obesity and diabetes, allergic disease to neurodevelopmental illnesses. Study (Youmans BP et al ., 2015) reveals that the composition of the gut microbiome in healthy, diarrhea-free travelers has characteristics of a dysbiotic gut, suggesting that these alterations could be associated with factors such as travel. During this study, Firmicutes:Bacteroidetes ratios in microbiome profile were observed to be high in the travelers who developed diarrhea. Experiments have also demonstrated that the use of antibiotics does have several short and long-term implications in the ecology of the normal gut microbiota (McFall-Ngai M et al ., 2007). It is proved that multi-drug resistant bacterial genes have been prevalent for thousands of years before the advent of antibiotics, which indicates an influence of exposure to small molecules from the environment with growth inhibitory properties. Researchers observed the intestinal microbiota of subjects who have undergone colonoscopy and it was confirmed that composition of the microbiota is disturbed in patients undergoing colonoscopy (V Mai et al ., 2006). Further to this study, it was found that the colonoscopy preparation did not significantly change the gut microbiota for the majority of subjects as determined by PCR-DGGE (Claire L. O'Brien et al ., 2013) and changes were as no greater than those seen in normal subjects over time. The link between gut microbiota variation and stool consistency was also investigated using Bristol Stool Scale classification, which reflects faecal water content and activity. This investigation (Doris Vandeputte et al ., 2015), has revealed a seemingly consistent association between disease and a reduction in the observed or estimated number of species in a sample, suggesting bacterial richness to be a major marker for gut health. The Prevotella (P) enterotype is more abundant in subjects with loose stool while the Ruminococcaceae-Bacteroides (RB) enterotype completely dominates the harder stool samples. QIIME is the most often used software to analyze 16S rRNA gene sequences from microbial Communities (Justin Kuczynski et al ., 2011 ). It is an acronym for Quantitative Insights Into Microbial Ecology. QIIME pipeline involves generating OTU table, picking up OTU representative, assigning taxonomic identities using reference database, creating a phylogenetic tree and producing alpha-beta diversity results.

3 Data

We have a longitudinal microbiota dataset taken from two individuals over a period of ten years. There are, in total, 89 samples that have undergone comprehensive 16S rRNA based microbiota sequencing. 16S rRNA sequencing is a common amplicon sequencing method that is useful in identifying and comparing bacteria within a given sample. Ribosomes, which are present in all living cells, function in protein synthesis machinery. Ribosomes consist of two subunits, each of which consists a protein and a type of RNA called ribosomal RNA(rRNA). Prokaryotic ribosomes consist of two subunits - 30S, the small subunit and 50S, the large subunit. While the 50S subunit is composed of two types of rRNA species, the 30S subunit consists of the 16S rRNA and 21 polynucleotide chains. The genes coding for the 16S rRNA are called 16S rRNA genes. The 16S rRNA gene has been widely used in the study of phylogeny and taxonomy for a number of reasons including - (i) its presence in almost all bacteria, often existing as a multigene family, or operons; (ii) the function of the 16S rRNA gene over time has not changed, suggesting that random sequence changes are a more accurate measure of time (evolution); and (iii) the 16S rRNA gene (1,500 bp) is large enough for informatics purposes. (Patel, J. B. et al.) (J. Michael Janda et al., 2007) The use of 16S rRNA genes in sequencing, therefore, helps to narrow down full length sequences to contain only 16S rRNA sequences which can then be used to identify specific microbes. In order to analyze microbial communities, our samples containing 16S rRNA sequences are assigned to Operational Taxonomic Units(OTUs). OTUs can be thought of as clusters that represent a taxonomic unit of bacteria species or genus. To assign sequences to OTUs, we first perform OTU Clustering based on similarity of the sequences. Typically, the similarity between a pair of sequences is computed as the percentage of sites that agree in a pairwise sequence alignment. A common similarity threshold used is 97%, which was derived from an empirical study that showed most strains had 97% 16S rRNA sequence similarity. (Konstantinidis et al., 2005) From each OTU cluster, a single sequence is selected as a representative sequence. This representative sequence is annotated and the annotation is applied to all the sequences within that OTU. We used three different OTU picking strategies - closed reference, DeNovo and open reference, all with QIIME. The closed reference approach involves comparing sequences against a reference sequence collection and then clustering sequences into the same OTU that are similar to the same reference sequence. Closed reference OTU picking is fully parallelizable and hence is extremely useful for large datasets. However, sequences that do not hit a sequence in the reference database are discarded and hence closed reference is not able to detect novel sequences. Defining OTUs in the closed-reference approach may also be problematic because two sequences might be 97% simila r to the same reference sequence, but they may only be 94% similar t o each other. (Sarah L. Westcott et al., 2015) Alternatively, a sequence may be equally similar to two or more reference sequences. In contrast, DeNovo clustering method uses a distance approach rather than referencebased. Sequences are clustered into an OTU based on the distances between them. DeNovo approach, therfore, clusters all the sequences but the computational cost scales quadratically with the number of unique sequences. The open-reference clustering is a combination of closedreference and denovo approaches. It clusters sequences against a reference database and sequences that do not hit a sequence, are denovo clustered. While it would seem that the open-reference approach would exploit strengths of both, the closed-reference and denovo approaches, it may run into problems due to different OTU definitons employed by the approaches. An alternative to this approach has been to classify sequences to a bacterial family or genus and then assign those sequences to OTUs within those taxonomic groups using the average linkage method. (Patrick D. Schloss et al., 2011)
